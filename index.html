Glaucoma, Explained Clearly

Protect vision.Understand it early.

Glaucoma is a group of eye diseases that damages the optic nerve, often slowly and with very few early symptoms. The goal of this page is simple: make the condition easy to understand, visually clear, and less intimidating.

Overview

What glaucoma actually is.

Glaucoma is not just "high eye pressure." It is optic nerve damage that can lead to permanent vision loss. Pressure inside the eye is an important risk factor, but the real concern is how that pressure, blood flow, and anatomy affect nerve tissue over time.

What is affected

The optic nerve carries visual information from the eye to the brain. When glaucoma damages that nerve, vision loss cannot be reversed.

Why it is missed

Symptoms can begin so gradually that people feel normal while vision is already narrowing at the edges.

Why exams matter

A comprehensive eye exam can find glaucoma before daily vision changes become obvious, which is the best time to act.

What Changes

The optic nerve is where glaucoma leaves its mark.

In glaucoma, damage builds gradually at the back of the eye. That is why doctors pay close attention to the optic nerve's shape, the retinal nerve fiber layer, and patterns of visual field loss. The condition is usually managed, not cured, so the earlier the pattern is recognized, the better.

1 Comprehensive dilated eye exam remains the key starting point.
2 Testing often includes pressure measurement plus visual field and optic nerve evaluation.

What doctors watch

Clinicians look for a pattern rather than a single number: optic nerve appearance, retinal nerve fiber changes, pressure trends, and visual field results together.

  • Optic disc structure
  • Retinal nerve fiber layer thickness
  • Peripheral vision performance over time
Care Pathway

How glaucoma is diagnosed and treated.

The aim is to reduce ongoing damage, usually by lowering eye pressure and monitoring the optic nerve over time. Treatment depends on the type and severity of glaucoma, but the logic is consistent: catch it, measure it, and slow it.

Pressure checks

Tonometry measures intraocular pressure. It is one part of the picture, not the whole diagnosis.

Visual field testing

This helps detect side-vision loss that a patient may not notice yet in everyday life.

Imaging and follow-up

Repeated optic nerve imaging helps track whether the disease appears stable or continues to progress.

Eye drops

Prescription drops are often used first to reduce eye pressure or improve fluid drainage.

Laser treatment

Laser procedures can improve outflow and help lower pressure with less recovery than major surgery.

Surgery

When needed, surgery creates or improves drainage pathways to better protect the optic nerve over time.

Disease Course

Three phases that explain how glaucoma is recognized and managed.

Glaucoma is often easiest to understand as a progression: early disease may be difficult to notice, testing makes change measurable, and treatment focuses on preserving vision over time.

1

Early Disease Can Be Subtle

Many patients have no pain and no obvious early symptoms. Vision can seem normal even while damage is beginning in the peripheral field.

Key point: glaucoma may progress before a person notices a meaningful change in daily vision.
2

Testing Makes Change Visible

Pressure measurements, optic nerve assessment, and visual field testing help convert a silent condition into something clinicians can monitor objectively.

Key point: diagnosis and follow-up usually depend on patterns seen across repeated exams, not on a single finding alone.
3

Long-Term Management Protects Vision

Treatment is designed to lower risk and slow progression, usually by reducing pressure and following the optic nerve over time.

Key point: the goal is long-term control and stability rather than a one-time cure.
Takeaway

Glaucoma is easier to manage when it is found before vision feels different.

If someone is at risk, has a family history, or has been told their eye pressure is high, the next practical step is a full eye exam with glaucoma-focused testing. Earlier clarity leads to better decisions.
