Eye anatomy

Glaucoma is damage in motion.

It is not just high eye pressure. Glaucoma is a group of diseases that progressively injure the optic nerve, often with few symptoms until permanent vision loss has already started.

Pressure inside the eye is one major risk factor, but the real clinical concern is how that pressure affects delicate nerve tissue over time.

That makes regular eye exams, visual field testing, and optic nerve imaging essential for catching disease earlier.

Visual field

Why glaucoma is called the silent thief of sight.

Vision loss often starts at the edges. Patients can feel as though they see normally while the field of view gradually narrows, which is why glaucoma can progress unnoticed.

Peripheral first

Damage commonly appears in side vision before affecting central detail.

Slow progression

Changes can accumulate gradually enough that the brain adapts and masks them.

Testing matters

Visual fields and optic nerve imaging help reveal loss that patients may not notice.

Treatment pathways

Modern care is about control, not delay.

The goal of treatment is to reduce pressure and preserve nerve function. The right plan depends on disease severity, anatomy, response to medication, and long-term monitoring.

Prescription drops

Often the first-line approach, eye drops lower intraocular pressure and are most effective when taken consistently.

△

Laser intervention

Laser procedures can improve drainage and reduce pressure without the recovery burden of larger surgery.

▣

Surgical management

For more advanced or difficult cases, surgery creates new drainage pathways to better stabilize pressure over time.
